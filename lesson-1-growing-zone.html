✦ The Basics

What Is a Growing Zone?

A growing zone — officially called a USDA Plant Hardiness Zone — is a number assigned to your region that tells you how cold it typically gets in winter. The U.S. is divided into 13 zones, each representing a 10°F range of average minimum winter temperatures.

Think of your zone as a shorthand for your climate. It tells you what plants can survive winter in your area and gives you a framework for timing your planting seasons. A gardener in Zone 4 (Minnesota) and a gardener in Zone 10 (Southern California) are working in completely different worlds — what grows, when it gets planted, and how long the season lasts are all different.

🗺️ The USDA Map Was Just Updated in 2023

For the first time in 11 years, the USDA released an updated zone map — and roughly half of the U.S. shifted to a warmer half-zone. If you last checked your zone more than a few years ago, it may have changed. Always check the current map.

Every plant sold at a nursery or in a seed catalog has a hardiness zone listed on its label. If your zone number is within or below that range, the plant should survive your winters. If it's above — the winters are too cold for that plant to come back year after year.

✦ Know Your Climate

The Three Main Zone Groups

While there are 13 official zones, most American gardeners fall into one of three broad climate groups. Here's what each means for your vegetable garden:

Cold Climates

Zones 3–5

Short growing seasons of 90–150 days. Winters are harsh with temps that can drop well below 0°F. Focus on cold-hardy crops and start seeds indoors early.

📍 Minnesota, Maine, Montana

Moderate Climates

Zones 6–8

Growing seasons of 150–250 days. True four seasons. You can grow most vegetables and have a productive spring and fall garden in addition to summer.

📍 Virginia, Kansas, Oregon

Warm Climates

Zones 9–11

Nearly year-round growing. Winters are mild with little to no frost. The challenge is summer heat — cool-season crops are planted in fall and winter instead.

📍 Florida, Texas, California

💡

Grower's Tip

Zones only tell you about winter cold. They don't account for summer heat, humidity, rainfall, or frost dates — all of which matter for vegetable gardening. Think of your zone as the starting point, not the whole picture.

✦ Take Action

How to Find Your Zone in 2 Minutes

Finding your zone is quick and free. Here's how to do it right now:

1

Go to the Official USDA Map

Visit planthardiness.ars.usda.gov — this is the official, most up-to-date source. It was last updated in 2023.

2

Enter Your Zip Code

Type in your zip code and the interactive map will zoom directly to your location and display your exact zone — down to the half-zone (e.g., 6b vs 6a).

3

Write It Down and Remember It

Your zone is something you'll reference constantly. Jot it in your garden journal, save it in your phone, or write it on the inside cover of any seed catalog you use.

4

Check for Microclimates

Your backyard might actually be slightly warmer or cooler than your official zone — especially if you're near a large body of water, in a valley, or in a dense urban area. Pay attention over time to how your specific spot behaves.

✦ Going Deeper

What Your Zone Doesn't Tell You

Zones are a great starting point, but they have real limits — especially for vegetable gardeners. Here's what your zone number won't tell you:

!

Your First & Last Frost Dates

Frost dates are what actually determine your planting calendar — not your zone. Two gardens in Zone 6 can have frost dates weeks apart. Look these up separately using your zip code on the Old Farmer's Almanac or the National Gardening Association's site.

!

Summer Heat & Humidity

Some vegetables struggle in intense summer heat regardless of your zone. A Zone 7 garden in humid Tennessee is very different from a Zone 7 garden in dry New Mexico. Know your summer conditions too.

!

Rainfall & Irrigation Needs

Your zone says nothing about how much it rains. A garden in the Pacific Northwest and one in Arizona can share a zone number but have completely different water needs.

🌱

The Bottom Line

Know your zone, but also talk to local gardeners and your county extension office. No map replaces the knowledge of someone who has been growing in your exact neighborhood for years.
